Jack Mallers is the Chicago-born founder and chief executive officer of Strike, a payments company built atop the bitcoin Lightning Network. He is, by 2025, in his early thirties, recognizable in any bitcoin conference photograph by his consistent baseball cap, his rapid speaking style, and the substantial likelihood that he will at some point on the stage say the phrase *my girl*, by which he means his fiancée, who features regularly in his public communications. He is also, by some measure, the single individual most responsible for translating bitcoin from a speculative asset into a payments rail in the consciousness of mainstream policymakers, including most consequentially the president of El Salvador.

Strike was founded in 2017 under the original name *Zap*, pivoting through several product iterations before launching in its current form in early 2020 as a Lightning-native payments app. The product proposition was straightforward: the user funded a Strike account in their local currency, the funds were converted to bitcoin and routed across the Lightning Network for cross-border transmission, and recipients in supported jurisdictions received local-currency payouts in seconds, at fees substantially below those charged by incumbent remittance providers. The technical complexity of the routing was hidden from both the sender and the receiver. The user experience was, intentionally, indistinguishable from any modern peer-to-peer payments application. The bitcoin underneath was an implementation detail.

Mallers's public emergence as a bitcoin figure tracked Strike's growth. He began speaking at bitcoin conferences in 2019, accumulated a Twitter following through a combination of Lightning evangelism and unpolished personal warmth, and by 2021 had become one of the bitcoin community's more recognizable younger figures. The defining moment in his public career was his appearance on the stage at the Bitcoin 2021 conference in Miami on June 5, 2021. He had been, in the weeks prior, working with the government of El Salvador on the legal-tender legislation that president Nayib Bukele would shortly announce. The announcement at Bitcoin 2021 — that El Salvador would make bitcoin legal tender alongside the dollar — was delivered through a video message from Bukele that Mallers introduced from the stage, in tears, having been the operational link between the bitcoin community and the Salvadoran government for the months preceding. The footage of Mallers's emotional introduction has become one of the most-circulated pieces of bitcoin community video in the asset's history.

Mallers and Strike have, in the years since, expanded the company's product set substantially. Strike now operates as a regulated money services business in dozens of jurisdictions, supports both bitcoin and dollar-denominated balances, integrates with traditional payment networks, and processes payments at a scale that, while still small relative to the major incumbent payment providers, is no longer trivial. The company's user count exceeds several million; its annual transaction volume exceeds tens of billions of dollars. The bitcoin community's relationship with Strike has been generally affectionate; the broader cryptocurrency community's has been mixed, primarily on the question of whether Strike's bitcoin-only positioning is excessively narrow. Mallers's public communications have, throughout, declined to apologize for the narrowness.

The cultural significance of Mallers, distinct from Strike's commercial fortunes, is that he has functioned as the bitcoin community's most effective liaison to the policymaking world. The El Salvador legal-tender adoption was the most visible product of that role, but it has not been the only one. Mallers has met with cabinet officials in multiple jurisdictions, advised on payments policy in Latin America and Africa, and become a recognizable presence in the regulatory conversation in Washington, D.C. His characteristic combination of technical fluency, evangelical conviction, and unpolished sincerity has, in those rooms, communicated something about the bitcoin community that more polished representatives have not consistently been able to. The *my girl* references and the baseball caps have not impeded the work; they have, in some respects, been the work. Whether bitcoin's mainstream adoption was inevitable or required a particular kind of human intermediary is a counterfactual question. Mallers has, in any event, been the intermediary the asset got, and the record of his interventions is now part of the catalog.
